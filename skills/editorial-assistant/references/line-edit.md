# Line Editing Checklist

Evaluate prose at the sentence and paragraph level. Flag specific passages — don't give general advice without examples.

## 1. Show vs. Tell

- [ ] Are emotions stated directly when they could be shown through action or detail?
  - **Tell**: "He was angry." **Show**: "He slammed the mug down. Coffee splashed across the report."
- [ ] Are character traits explained instead of demonstrated?
  - **Tell**: "She was brave." **Show**: "She stepped in front of the barrel."
- [ ] Is backstory dumped in exposition instead of woven into scene?
- [ ] Are stakes explained by the narrator instead of felt through conflict?

## 2. Sentence Rhythm & Variety

- [ ] Are sentences monotonously the same length?
- [ ] Is there a mix of simple, compound, and complex sentences?
- [ ] Do short sentences create impact at key moments?
- [ ] Do longer sentences slow the pace intentionally (description, reflection)?
- [ ] Are sentence openings varied (not all starting with "He...", "She...", "The...")?
- [ ] Does the rhythm match the content — fast for action, measured for reflection?

## 3. Voice & Tone

- [ ] Is the voice consistent throughout?
- [ ] Does the narrative voice match the genre and intended audience?
- [ ] Is the tone appropriate for the subject matter?
- [ ] If multiple POVs, does each have a distinct voice?
- [ ] Does the voice feel authentic, not performative?

## 4. Word Choice

- [ ] Are there vague or generic words where specific ones would be stronger?
  - "Walked" vs. "shuffled", "strode", "limped"
  - "Nice" vs. "warm", "generous", "calculated"
- [ ] Are the same words or phrases repeated distractingly?
- [ ] Are there clichés that could be replaced with fresh language?
- [ ] Is jargon appropriate for the audience?
- [ ] Are modifiers (very, really, quite, rather) earning their place or diluting?

## 5. Filter Words

Flag instances of filter words that distance the reader from the experience:

- **Sensory filters**: felt, heard, saw, noticed, watched, realized, seemed, appeared
- **Cognitive filters**: thought, knew, understood, wondered, decided, remembered
- **Emotional filters**: felt happy, felt sad, felt scared

**Instead of**: "He felt the cold wind on his face."
**Consider**: "The wind bit his face."

## 6. Passive Voice

- [ ] Is passive voice used where active would be stronger?
  - **Passive**: "The door was opened by Sarah." **Active**: "Sarah opened the door."
- [ ] Is passive voice used deliberately for effect (hiding the actor, emphasizing the object)?
- [ ] Note: Passive voice is sometimes correct — flag it, don't automatically condemn it.

## 7. Dialogue

- [ ] Does dialogue sound like people actually talk (contractions, interruptions, incomplete thoughts)?
- [ ] Can you tell who's speaking without the dialogue tag?
- [ ] Are dialogue tags mostly "said/asked" (invisible) rather than "exclaimed/declared/opined"?
- [ ] Are action beats used alongside dialogue to ground the scene?
  - **Without**: "I don't trust him," she said.
  - **With**: "I don't trust him." She set down the glass and didn't look up.
- [ ] Does dialogue serve a purpose (reveal character, advance plot, create tension)?
- [ ] Are there talking-head scenes — long stretches of dialogue without grounding?

## 8. Transitions

- [ ] Are paragraph transitions smooth?
- [ ] Are there abrupt topic shifts that lose the reader?
- [ ] Are transition phrases overused ("However," "Moreover," "In addition")?
- [ ] Do paragraphs follow a logical flow within sections?

## 9. Economy

- [ ] Are there redundant phrases ("He nodded his head", "She shrugged her shoulders")?
- [ ] Are there unnecessary adverbs modifying strong verbs ("He ran quickly")?
- [ ] Can any sentences be cut without losing meaning?
- [ ] Are there throat-clearing openings that delay the point?
- [ ] Is every word earning its place?

## 10. Paragraph Construction

- [ ] Does each paragraph have a clear focus?
- [ ] Are paragraphs varied in length?
- [ ] Are single-sentence paragraphs used for emphasis (not as a crutch)?
- [ ] Do paragraphs end on strong notes?
